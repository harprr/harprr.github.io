How did forest land change from 1990 to 2015 in developing countries

Harper DING Chang 2018.3.2

FAO has done research on the change of forest land in different country groups, which are Land Locked Developing Countries, Least Developing Countries, Land Locked Development Countries, Small Island Developing Countries, Low income Food Deficit Countries and Net Food Importing Developing Countries from 1990 to 2015. The findings is shown on the graph below.

As shown in the chart, forest land in most developing countries have been reducing. However, in one certain group of developing countries, the forest land has had a slight increase compared to the 1990s. That is the small island developing states(SIDS). Why do they have reverse circumstances with other developing countries that are trying to make the biggest use of every inch of land for industry or commercialization?

Increase in forest land as a country group doesn't mean all SIDS countries have well preserved the forest. As a group, SIDS possess extensive forests. However, due to a considerable variation in land area,1 population density and climatic, geological and topographical conditions, the extent of forest cover differs greatly among the island states.

High deforestation rate have been seen in states like Belize and Haiti. The main causes of deforestation include conversion of forests into agricultural land and infrastructure development. Forest degradation can also be caused by natural causes.

While Cape Verde, Cuba, Grenada and Vanuatu registered a positive change in forest cover from 1990 to 2000, mainly as a result of afforestation efforts.

Among SIDS with limited forest cover, trees outside forests (such as on agricultural land) often play a very important role in local livelihoods. It is an unquantified, undervalued resource, which nevertheless often has very significant local value. Many small island nations, for instance, have abundant coconut trees, which serve as sources of wood, coconut, copra and palm oil for the local population.
